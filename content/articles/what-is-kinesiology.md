> **Quick answer:** Kinesiology is a body-based practice that uses muscle monitoring (gentle pressure on a muscle, usually your arm) to identify where your body is holding stress, tension, or emotional patterns. Once found, targeted corrections from Chinese medicine, neuroscience, and somatic processing help your body release what it's been carrying.

## What happens in a kinesiology session

You lie fully clothed on a treatment table. The kinesiologist applies gentle pressure to your arm while introducing different stressors, physical, emotional, or biochemical. Your muscle response changes when a stressor is relevant to what your body is holding.

This is called **muscle monitoring**, and it's the foundation of all kinesiology work. It's not painful. Most people find it calming.

## What does kinesiology help with?

Kinesiology is most effective for people experiencing:

- **Anxiety that lives in the body** — chest tightness, shallow breathing, constant tension
- **Stress that won't shift** — despite rest, exercise, meditation, or therapy
- **Emotional patterns on repeat** — the same reactions, overwhelm, or shutdown
- **Physical tension with no clear medical cause** — headaches, jaw clenching, digestive issues

## How is it different from other therapies?

Most therapies work through the mind — talking, cognitive reframing, behavioural change. Kinesiology works through the body directly. It finds what your body is holding without requiring you to consciously identify or articulate it first.

This is why it often helps people who have "tried everything else." The stress they're carrying isn't accessible through thinking or talking. It's stored in muscle tension, movement patterns, and nervous system responses that need a body-based approach to shift.

## How many sessions will I need?

It depends on what you're working with. Some people feel significant shifts in 2-3 sessions. Deeper or long-held patterns often need 6-8 sessions. A good kinesiologist will be honest about this after the first session.

## Is kinesiology evidence-based?

Kinesiology draws from Chinese medicine meridian theory, anatomy, neuroscience, and somatic processing. The muscle monitoring technique has peer-reviewed research supporting its reliability, though the field is still building its evidence base compared to established medical disciplines.

What most practitioners and clients will tell you: the changes are felt, measurable, and often noticed by others before the client fully registers them.

## Finding a kinesiologist in Melbourne

If you're in Melbourne, look for a practitioner qualified in **Professional Kinesiology Practice (PKP)**, the most comprehensive training system available. Ask about their approach to trauma (it should be informed and consent-based) and whether they offer initial sessions long enough to do meaningful work (75 minutes minimum).
